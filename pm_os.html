The PM OS

My personal philosophies for an operating system for leaders and project managers.

This is a living document: prone to revisions and additions. Still being pressure tested.

Building a system of trust

When trust is high, you can focus on team direction and the future. When trust is low, you become concerned with all objects outside your control.

Trust must be learned.
Trust = (Delivery * Quality) ^ Repetitions
It compounds and one fuck up tanks it.

To the team: Be perceptive of how others complete tasks. Though harsh, you must look at results not intentions. The expectation is that they communicate difficulties.

From the team: Deliver on promises 100% of the time, with speed and quality. Be apologetic if you fall short. Your tasks are few compared to theirs, this is opportunity to set the standard.

Communication channels

You don't want a unidirectional information channel. It goes both ways:

  • You allocate tasks, give feedback on results and check in on progress
  • They ask questions about tasks, communicate concerns and ask for advice

Information when allocating the task: balance between precision in expectation and annoyance
Information during work: avoid catastrophic task collapse but maintain their autonomy
Information after completion: feedback for future tasks is important

Avoid hierarchy in communication. Information obeys vertical gravity: heavy coming down and difficult to rise.

If you cannot confidently get a brief response within the hour, fix the channel. Feel free set up different channels for different urgency.

Meetings

Meetings are useful insofar as they block off time in everyone's calendar.

Waste zero minutes. The shorter the better, always.

As PM you must engineer the schedule. Set goals. Bring the conversation back when it's offtrack.

Structure:

  1. Confirm time day-of. Kill if unnecessary.
  2. Start with aligning everyone's vectors at a personal level. We're all human. Brief and genuine roundtable.
  3. Summarize meeting goals.
  4. State of the now. Honest facts, not opinion.
  5. Open floor.
  6. Expectations for next meeting. Restate tasks.

Decision making

The system is as follows:

tension between desire and current state (input) → judgement → proposal for resolution (output)

Success usually resides in a few key decisions. Get those right. They are yours to judge.

Most decisions don't matter. They still need to happen, however. Allocate decision-making strategically to encourage confidence/ownership.

Define the boundary for decisions before they are on the table.

When there's disagreement, agreement isn't required to move on. You can adopt something while disagreeing with it (as long as you trust the person).

Before a decision is finalized be receptive to input. The system is recursive. Once it's finalized, it's stone. Cross it off.

Conflict resolution

Conflict is inevitable. Even well-engineered machines get hot.

Interpersonal:
Between people. Ego, style, personal friction. Handle privately, directly, fast. Don't let it fester into camps.

Directional:
Disagreement on approach, priority, or vision. This one's actually healthy if contained properly. Surface it, debate it, decide it, close it. The danger is when it goes unresolved and people silently pull in different directions (at which point it can grow into interpersonal).

Don't get the two confused. Handle them differently.

Fault does not exist. It's not a useful abstraction for teams. Responsibility trumps.

You don't need to make everyone like each other. You need to keep friction from blocking output.
